circuits

All electrical phenomena are explained by Maxwell's equations, but we need to introduce some quantities and concepts that will make it easier for us to translate how the physics of the previous section governs the properties and dynamics of circuits. First let's talk about voltage. Voltage is also known as electromotive force (emf), however it is not demensionally equivalent to force. But it does act analogous to a force. A voltage or emf will cause free charges to move. It is the electric field that is actually producing a force, \(F\), on a free charge, \(q\), according to \(F = Eq\), and the voltage is the line integral of the electric field along the path of a circuit. But those equations aren't very useful for describing circuits and you should focus on the concept of voltage. Confusingly, there is another term for voltage or emf and that is electric potential difference.

Ohm's law

A voltage, \(V\), across a circuit will cause free charges to move across it. Moving charges form a current, \(I\), so a voltage produces a current. Free charges aren't completely free to move across a circuit. The circuit will resist the free movement of charges. This property is called resistance, \(R\), and can be quantified. The relationship between the voltage, current, and resistance across a circuit is given by Ohm's law

\begin{equation}V=IR\tag{4}\end{equation}

There are a couple of interesting things to note about Ohm's law. Typically the resistance is a constant property of the circuit. The voltage is directly proportional to the current. So if you increase the voltage you apply across a circuit you will increase the current that flows through it. But if you apply the same voltage to a circuit with a larger resistance you won't be able to produce as large of a current. Current and resistance are inversely proportional. If you apply a voltage across a circuit with a very small resistance, the current will be very large. In the ideal limit, as the resistance goes toward zero, the current will rise to infinity. High currents are damaging and dangerous. If you apply a voltage across a circuit with a small resistance, it is likely to heat up and eventually break. It also could injure you if you touch it. Sometimes something conducting might touch a functioning circuit and bypass the sections of the circuit that have an appropriate resistance. This is called a short circuit and will probably cause your circuit to break.

Fuses are often used to protect circuits from reaching dangerous current levels. A fuse is a resistive element that is placed in a circuit and is made of a weak material by design. If the current rises too high it will heat up and destroy the fuse. The location of the fuse will then be a gap in the circuit and no current will be allowed to flow even though there is a voltage applied. This leads us to another concept. A circuit must be closed for current to flow. You could say that the gap in the blown fuse actually means that the fuse now has an infinite resistance and that regardless of how high the voltage is, the current must be zero according to Ohm's law. But a gap in a circuit does not actually have an infinite resistance just a very high one. The resistance between the moisture high up in the atmosphere and the ground is very high. However, during a thunderstorm, the voltage between the clouds and the ground may increase enough and the resistance of the air may go down enough that the atmosphere becomes a closed instead of an open circuit and current flows from the atmosphere to the ground. Lightning strikes.
